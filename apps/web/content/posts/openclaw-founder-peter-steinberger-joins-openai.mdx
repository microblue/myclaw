# OpenClaw Founder Peter Steinberger Joins OpenAI: What It Means for the Project

On February 14, 2026, Peter Steinberger — the Austrian developer behind OpenClaw, one of the most popular open-source projects on GitHub — announced that he is joining OpenAI. The news sent shockwaves through the developer community, sparking debate about the future of open-source AI agents and what happens when a beloved project's creator moves to Big Tech.

Here's everything you need to know.

## What Happened

In a blog post titled "OpenClaw, OpenAI and the future," Steinberger confirmed that he is joining OpenAI to work on **bringing AI agents to everyone**. His stated goal: build an agent that "even my mum can use."

The announcement came after a week in San Francisco where Steinberger met with major AI labs, gaining access to unreleased research and exploring opportunities. Ultimately, he felt OpenAI was the best fit for his vision.

"What I want is to change the world, not build a large company," Steinberger wrote. "Teaming up with OpenAI is the fastest way to bring this to everyone."

## What Happens to OpenClaw

This is the question everyone asked first: **Is OpenClaw dead?**

The answer is no. Steinberger made several key commitments:

- **OpenClaw will move to a foundation** — a formal structure to ensure its independence
- **It stays open source** — no license changes, no proprietary pivot
- **OpenAI is already sponsoring the project** — financial backing to keep development going
- **The foundation will support multiple models and companies** — not just OpenAI

In Steinberger's words: "It will stay a place for thinkers, hackers and people that want a way to own their data."

## Why OpenAI

Steinberger was clear about his reasoning. He previously founded PSPDFKit and spent 13 years building that company. He's not interested in repeating that journey with OpenClaw.

Instead, he wants access to frontier AI research and the resources to push agents into the mainstream. OpenAI offers both — the latest models, a massive user base, and the infrastructure to ship at scale.

"The more I talked with the people there, the clearer it became that we both share the same vision," he wrote.

## How the Community Reacted

The announcement hit Hacker News and accumulated over **1,400 points and more than 1,100 comments** in just two days — making it one of the most discussed posts of the week.

The reaction was deeply split.

### The Optimists

Many celebrated the move as validation for the open-source AI agent ecosystem. OpenClaw had already amassed over 190,000 GitHub stars, making it the 21st most popular repository on the platform. Steinberger joining OpenAI could mean more resources, better models, and faster development for everyone building with agents.

The foundation model also reassured developers. Open-source projects that depend on a single maintainer are fragile — a foundation with corporate sponsorship is a much more sustainable path.

### The Skeptics

Others were less enthusiastic. A recurring theme on Hacker News was concern about **OpenClaw's security track record**. As TechCrunch reported just days earlier, security researchers had found significant vulnerabilities in OpenClaw-powered applications, including the Moltbook social network where credentials were left unsecured.

Some commenters questioned whether the hire was based on merit or virality:

> "We have someone who vibe coded software with major security vulnerabilities... Would you hire someone who never read any of the code that they've developed? Well, this is what happened here."

Others pushed back, arguing that the ability to ship products that people actually use matters more than code purity:

> "You don't get hired for the best positions for being a 10x programmer who excels at hackerrank. You get hired for your proven ability to deliver useful products."

### The Pragmatists

A third camp acknowledged both sides. OpenClaw may not be breaking new scientific ground — as AI engineer Artem Sorokin told TechCrunch, "From an AI research perspective, this is nothing novel" — but it hit a **capability threshold** by organizing existing tools into something seamless and accessible.

The real question isn't whether Steinberger deserves the role. It's whether OpenClaw can thrive without its creator at the helm.
